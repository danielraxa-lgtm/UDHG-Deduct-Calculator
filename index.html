What is an OCIP/CCIP? (Plain English)

What is a "Wrap-Up" Program?

A Wrap-Up is a consolidated insurance program where one party (the Owner or GC) provides insurance coverage for all contractors working on a project. Instead of every contractor bringing their own insurance, it's all "wrapped up" into one program.

Think of it like this:

Imagine you're going to a potluck dinner. Normally, everyone brings their own dish (their own insurance). But sometimes the host says "Don't bring anything‚ÄîI'm providing all the food" (the owner or GC provides all the insurance). That's a wrap-up program.

OCIP = Owner Controlled Insurance Program (the Owner is the "host")
CCIP = Contractor Controlled Insurance Program (the GC is the "host")

Since they're covering our insurance on that job, we give them a credit (deduction) in our bid price. We're basically saying "Here's what we would have spent on insurance for this job‚Äîtake it off our price since you're covering it."

  • Are we self-installing, using subs, or delivery only?
  • What state is the project in?

Why Payroll Tracking Matters

The "Double Insurance" Problem (Plain English)

Imagine you pay for a gym membership, but then your employer also pays for the same gym membership for you. You're paying twice for the same thing. That's what happens when we don't track OCIP hours.

Here's the deal:

  • Normally: We pay workers' comp insurance on ALL our payroll
  • On OCIP jobs: The owner/GC is supposed to cover workers' comp for work on their project
  • The problem: If we don't tell our insurance carrier which hours were OCIP hours, they charge us for those hours too
  • Result: We're paying for insurance that someone else already covered = throwing money away
üí∞ The Good News: If we track OCIP payroll hours properly, we report them to our carrier at the end of the policy year and they give us money back (or reduce what we owe). This can be thousands of dollars per project.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between an OCIP and a CCIP?
Both are "wrap-up" insurance programs. OCIP = Owner controls it. CCIP = General Contractor controls it. Either way, they provide the insurance and we give them a credit in our bid.
How do I know if a project is a wrap job?
Check the bid documents or ask the GC. If they mention "OCIP," "CCIP," "wrap-up insurance," or ask for an insurance deduction, it's a wrap job.
What's the difference between UDHG and FBM on wrap projects?
FBM is supply-only‚Äîno installation, no labor exposure. UDHG may include installation work, which means we could have workers' comp exposure depending on the scope.
Should I use labor cost or labor sell for the WC calculation?
Always use labor cost (payroll). Never labor sell. Insurance is based on exposure, not what we charge. Using sell price gives away our profit to the GC.
What if we're subcontracting the install or it's delivery-only?
Select "Subcontracted" or "None" in the calculator. WC will be $0 because that exposure belongs to the sub or doesn't exist.
What's a typical deduction on a small change order?
Usually $0 to $1 for material-only. $1 to $10 for light install labor. Larger installs scale with payroll. Small amounts round down because GL and Umbrella only add a few cents.
Which states is UDHG approved for self-install?
AZ, CO, FL, MD, MI, NJ, NY, PA, and TX. If you've got a self-install project in another state, contact Risk Management before bidding.
Why do we need to track OCIP payroll hours?
Because we're already paying workers' comp on all our payroll. If we don't report which hours were covered by the OCIP, we're paying double. Tracking lets us get money back from our carrier.
What information does the wrap admin need for enrollment?
W-9, FEIN, NCCI ID, current EMR (varies by state), rate pages for GL/Umbrella/WC, project name, contract value, scope of work, and jobsite address.
Who handles enrollment?
Risk Management. Once Contracts confirms it's a wrap job, they hand it off to Risk Management for enrollment. Contact: wrap.enrollment@myfbm.com
